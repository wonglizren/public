Taṇhā / TUN-ha · thirst /

Craving, grasping, clinging. Grips you and never stays full — feed it, and it just wants more.

Chanda / CHUN-da · aspiration /

Skillful drive: to learn, help, grow, be free. Moves you forward — and lets go when it's done. You need it to walk any path worth walking.

the first teaching · SN 56.11

Craving is the cause
of suffering.

2,500 years ago, in his very first talk, the Buddha named that restless, never-quite-enough ache — dukkha — and pointed at its engine.

Not pain itself. The craving for things to be other than they are: the second arrow you fire at yourself.

So the next question is simple: why does craving hurt?

why craving burns · tap to reveal

Four ways the grip hurts.

🪤

Leaning on it for lasting happiness

You hand your peace to a thing that can't hold it — so the calm always leaks back out.

🤲

Clinging to the pleasant

You try to freeze a good moment. But moments move — and the squeeze is the ache.

🙅

Resisting the unpleasant

Pushing away what's already here just doubles it. Fighting reality is exhausting.

🪞

Building "me" around it

Car, grades, followers, the relationship — make them you, and losing them feels like dying.

That's why it's compared to fire: restless, grabbing, unstable — feed it and it only grows. One want fulfilled, the next appears.

the root · tap to reveal

So why crave at all?

Because we don't clearly see three things about everything:

🌊

Anicca

impermanence

Nothing lasts. So clinging is gripping water — guaranteed to slip.

🕳️

Dukkha

unsatisfactoriness

No thing can fully, finally satisfy. The "enough" you're chasing isn't out there.

🌀

Anatta

no fixed self

There's no solid, separate "me" that owning more will complete.

Miss these, and craving runs on autopilot. See them, and the grip starts to loosen on its own.

now the twist

There's an economy
built to keep you thirsty.

Consumerism isn't evil — it's an engine. And it runs on the exact thing we just studied: craving, dissatisfaction, and identity.

If you were never satisfied, you'd keep buying. So a whole system gets very good at making sure you're never quite satisfied.

so… is owning things wrong?

No. The stuff isn't
the problem — the grip is.

The middle way: enjoy without clinging. A few moves that un-feed the fire:

Pause before you buy

One breath. Ask: is this chanda (a real, useful want) or taṇhā (a manufactured itch)?

Use it, don't become it

Own the phone; don't let the phone own your sense of who you are.

Notice the fade

Remember how fast the last "must-have" got boring. That memory is freedom.

Practice gratitude

Contentment is the one thing the ad can't sell you — and it cools the fire.

bonus · the trap with no lock

The monkey trap.

✊

A hollow gourd is tied down. Something sweet sits inside. The hole fits only an open hand.

The monkey reaches in and grabs the treat. Now its fist is too big to pull back out.

It pulls. It panics. It's stuck. But the trap has no lock, no net, no door.

It's held by its own grip. Freedom is one decision away: let go.
