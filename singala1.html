the scene · early morning, ancient india

A young man, soaking wet,
bowing to the sky.

SIṄGĀLA

A young man named Siṅgāla gets up at dawn, wet hair, wet clothes, and bows to the six directions — east, south, west, north, down, up.

Why? Because his father, as he lay dying, asked him to. He isn't sure what it means — he just knows his father asked, and he honours that.

The Buddha walks past and asks the obvious question: "Why are you doing that?"

The Buddha's answer is the interesting one. He doesn't say "stop bowing." He says: you're bowing to the wrong thing. Not the sky — the people.
the twist

Same ritual. New meaning.

before · empty ritual

Bow to six directions in the sky.

Get wet. Face east. Repeat because you were told to. Nothing in your life actually changes. Nobody is better off.

after · real practice

Each direction is a person in your life.

"Honouring" them isn't bowing — it's how you actually treat them. Do that, and the six directions become a full, protected life.

YOU

Because six directions is all of space — north, south, east, west, plus above and below. Stand anywhere and point: you cannot find a seventh.

Fill all six with real relationships, and nobody who matters is left out.

Siṅgāla bowed to empty space to stay safe; the Buddha told him it's the people in those directions who actually protect him.

the part people miss

It goes both ways.

This isn't a list of rules for kids to obey. For every duty you have, the sutta gives the other person duties back. Care is a two-way street — even with parents and teachers.

you → your parents

What you give

  • Support them as they age
  • Do your part in the family
  • Keep the family's good name
  • Be worthy of their trust
⇢⇠
your parents → you

What they give back

  • Keep you from harm
  • Support your education
  • Teach you what's right
  • Hand you your independence

Read that right column again. The sutta expects care from them too. That's radical — then and now.

zooming in · the north direction

Let's talk about
your friends.

Of the six, this is the one you spend the most hours inside — and the one nobody hands you a manual for.

The sutta is blunt about it. It says some people look like friends but aren't. And it tells you exactly how to spot them.

the four fakes
🫴The taker — always takes, never gives back.
💬The talker — big promises, zero follow-through.
🪞The flatterer — cheers you on even when you're wrong; praises you to your face, trashes you behind your back.
🎲The reckless one — only shows up for the trouble.
the true-hearted
💚Helps you, stays through the bad, tells you the truth, actually wants your good.
your life · discuss, then tap to compare

Three real situations.

SITUATION 01
Your friend is being teased online.
Straight from the text: a true friend protects you when you're careless, stands by you in trouble — and the compassionate friend stops others from speaking ill of you. Not a public speech. A DM, sitting with them, refusing to laugh along. Silence is a choice too.
SITUATION 02
Big assignment due. Friends want you to skip it and game.
Notice which friend this is. The reckless companion shows up only for the fun. A true friend wants what's actually good for you — and would tell you to go study. What kind of friend are you being here?
SITUATION 03
Your parents ask for help with a household task.
The east direction. "Do your duties in the family" is literally in the text. Not because you're a servant — because you're part of something that holds you up. And their side of the care runs back to you.

Talk it through before you tap. Your answer matters more than the sutta's.
